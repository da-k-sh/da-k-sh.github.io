Reading List & Resources

A curated list of textbooks, papers, and lecture notes that I find valuable. Roughly ordered by accessibility within each section.

Lattice-Based Cryptography

Introductory

  • Oded Regev — Lattices in Computer Science (Lecture Notes) — The standard starting point for lattices and LWE. Clear and well-paced.
  • Daniele Micciancio & Oded Regev — Lattice-based Cryptography (Post-Quantum Cryptography, 2009) — Good survey chapter covering the basics of lattice problems and their cryptographic applications.
  • Chris Peikert — A Decade of Lattice Cryptography (2016) — Excellent survey that covers the landscape up to ~2015. Dense but rewarding.

Advanced

  • Daniele Micciancio & Shafi Goldwasser — Complexity of Lattice Problems: A Cryptographic Perspective — The definitive reference for hardness results and reductions.
  • Chris Peikert — An Efficient and Parallel Gaussian Sampler for Lattices (CRYPTO 2010) — Essential reading for understanding Gaussian sampling in lattice schemes.

Fully Homomorphic Encryption

Introductory

  • Craig Gentry — A Fully Homomorphic Encryption Scheme (PhD Thesis, 2009) — The original. Chapter 1 alone is worth reading for the intuition.
  • Zvika Brakerski, Craig Gentry & Vinod Vaikuntanathan — (Leveled) Fully Homomorphic Encryption without Bootstrapping (BGV, 2012) — Introduced modulus switching. Foundational for understanding modern FHE.
  • Junfeng Fan & Frederik Vercauteren — Somewhat Practical Fully Homomorphic Encryption (BFV, 2012) — The scheme behind my BFV library. Practical and well-written.

CKKS & Approximate HE

  • Cheon, Kim, Kim & Song — Homomorphic Encryption for Arithmetic of Approximate Numbers (CKKS, 2017) — The CKKS paper. Key to understanding HE for machine learning applications.
  • Cheon et al. — A Full RNS Variant of Approximate Homomorphic Encryption (2018) — The RNS optimization that makes CKKS practical.

Advanced / Implementation

  • Halevi & Shoup — Algorithms in HElib (2014) — If you want to understand how FHE is actually implemented efficiently.
  • Craig Gentry & Shai Halevi — Implementing Gentry's Fully-Homomorphic Encryption Scheme (2011) — The first real implementation paper. Shows the gap between theory and practice.

Cryptanalysis

  • Daksh Pandey — Cryptanalysis of Lattice-Based Cryptography (Work in Progress) — My own book. Available here.
  • Phong Nguyen & Brigitte Vallée — The LLL Algorithm: Survey and Applications — Comprehensive treatment of LLL and its role in cryptanalysis.
  • Martin Albrecht et al. — On the concrete hardness of Learning with Errors (2015) — Essential for understanding practical security estimates of LWE-based schemes.

Type Theory

Introductory

  • Benjamin Pierce — Types and Programming Languages (TAPL) — The standard intro. Covers simple types through System F with great exercises.
  • Morten Heine Sørensen & Pawel Urzyczyn — Lectures on the Curry-Howard Isomorphism — Bridges logic and type theory. A great companion to TAPL.

Homotopy Type Theory & Dependent Types

  • The Univalent Foundations Program — Homotopy Type Theory: Univalent Foundations of Mathematics (The HoTT Book) — Free and collaborative. Challenging but foundational.
  • Per Martin-Löf — Intuitionistic Type Theory (Bibliopolis Notes, 1984) — The source. Read this after you have some background.

Category Theory

  • Saunders Mac Lane — Categories for the Working Mathematician — The classic. Best read with some algebraic background.
  • Emily Riehl — Category Theory in Context — More modern and arguably more accessible than Mac Lane. Free PDF available from the author.
  • Steve Awodey — Category Theory — Good middle ground between introductory and advanced. Pairs well with type theory study.

Abstract Algebra & Number Theory

  • Dummit & Foote — Abstract Algebra — The standard reference. Comprehensive and well-organized.
  • Artin — Algebra — More geometric intuition than Dummit & Foote. Great for a second perspective.
  • Ireland & Rosen — A Classical Introduction to Modern Number Theory — Bridges classical and algebraic number theory. Relevant for understanding the Legendre symbol and related constructions.
  • Koblitz — A Course in Number Theory and Cryptography — Directly connects number theory to cryptographic applications.

This list reflects my personal recommendations as of 2026. It is not exhaustive — just what I've found most useful.
